1888 – 1890s the building neighborhood

Built on a Farm

Sometime between 1888 and 1889, on what was once a farm owned by William Covert, an ornate four-story brick building went up at 1643 Broadway. The elevated train stations at Chauncey Street and Halsey Street had opened a few summers before, and the neighborhood was changing fast.

Broadway was still cobblestone. Streetcars ran on tracks pulled by horses. A couple blocks up at Covert and Evergreen stood Roth farm, where neighbors fetched well-water and kids stashed scrap wood for bonfires. Ol' Fink's Pond on Central Ave was a swimming hole in summer and a skating pond in winter.

"When all were in place we set fire to them. And was that a blaze — it even awakened Red, the cop, who tried to chase the entire neighborhood away."

— Brooklyn Daily Eagle, on a neighborhood Christmas tree bonfire, c.1885

Near Myrtle Ave, kids gathered fruit from orchards in fall and built fires to roast spuds in winter. They played baseball in empty fields, stole rides on the Canarsie train, and watched the orchard trees come down as the L went up overhead. Then one spring, Mr. Grassman began building rows of houses where they had played and cows had grazed.

1889 – 1920s bohack's the building

Bohack's Corner Grocery

First business to take the corner at 1643 Broadway was H.C. Bohack Co., Grocers. Henry C. Bohack had immigrated to Brooklyn from Cuxhaven, Germany at seventeen, worked as a clerk for $15 a month, then opened his first store at Lorimer Street and Broadway on May 25, 1889 — marrying his wife the very next day. They lived above the shop.

By 1903, the corner of Covert and Broadway was one of ten "completely equipped modern stores" marketed as Brooklyn's quality grocer. They advertised coffee constantly:

"Paramount Blend Coffee. Perfection in quality; perfect aroma; cannot be equalled anywhere for this price. 5 lbs. for $1.15."

— The Chat, Mar 12, 1910. (Adjusted for inflation: $36.28 in 2024.)

The Bohacks became prominent Brooklynites. On August 18, 1893, after a Cooper Street fire left 25 families homeless, H.C. Bohack provided groceries free of charge to everyone affected. When the couple returned from an eight-month trip to Europe in 1901, two hundred employees and associates waited at Stanwix Hall — a full band serenaded them outside, then a genuine German supper was enjoyed.

Bohack's brand survived until 1977, even appearing in episode 66 of The Sopranos.

1893 – 1940s neighborhood clippings

Neighborhood Stories

The blocks around 1643 Broadway were dense with life — and the local papers recorded everything. A boy highwayman on Covert Street. A mob and a fire on Cooper Street. Vending machines looted on Chauncey. A kidnapping at Halsey. Cooper Street was always noisy. Life was never quiet here.

"BOY HIGHWAYMAN? Patrolman Russel pressed a charge of robbery against Frank Bundy... The youngster held up two small boys from Ridgewood and robbed them of eleven cents and a penknife."

— Brooklyn Times Union, Jun 25, 1909

Next door at 1645–47, W.J. O'Shaughnessy built the H-O Drug Co. into "an institution — everyone goes there for all kinds of medicine and drug store supplies." Among the first in the area to advertise delivery by automobile. At 7 Covert Street, the Bushwick Realty Co. ran its office in 1923. The corner of Broadway and Covert has always had something going on.

1920s – 1960s the building clippings

Flower Market, Fruit Stand, Luncheonette

During the 1920s, 1643 Broadway appears in newspaper ads as one of five locations of "JAY PEE Flower Markets." The flower market advertised here for two years before a fruit and vegetable market took over — visible in the first known photograph of the building, taken in the 1940s.

Apartments above the shop were always on the market: 1916 — six rooms and a bath, rent $17 (about $487 today). 1926 — four-to-five rooms, $20–$25.

"LUNCHEONETTE — CUSTARD STORE; corner location; quick sale account of sickness. 1643 Broadway."

— Brooklyn Daily Eagle classified, July 1, 1950

A few years later, a grocery took the space. In 1957, The Williamsburg News reported a license issued "to sell beer at retail in a grocery store… at 1643 Broadway, Brooklyn, for off premises consumption."

1940s – 1980s photos the building

Photographs of the Block

The 1980s were rock bottom for the Broadway-Bushwick corridor. Buildings boarded up, vacant, or used as shooting galleries. What NBC described in 1976 as "a part of Brooklyn that few people see anymore… a battleground for the police and young street criminals." Local gangs called it the "burning bush."

2015 – present covert coffee

Covert Coffee

Aaron Mounier — a New Yorker who had spent his life between the Bronx and Queens — found 1643 Broadway for sale on Craigslist around 2015. He saw something in the corner that decades of hard years hadn't extinguished. The building still held the story.

Covert Coffee opened and immediately became a neighbourhood anchor. Raw, intimate, unmistakably Brooklyn. The original iteration is documented below.
